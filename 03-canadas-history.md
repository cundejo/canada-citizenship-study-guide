# Chapter 3: Canada's History

## Early History

- Aboriginal peoples have lived in Canada for **thousands of years**
- **Vikings (Norse)** — Reached Labrador and Newfoundland ~1,000 years ago; settlement at **L'Anse aux Meadows** is a UNESCO World Heritage Site
- **John Cabot (1497)** — First to map Canada's Atlantic shore; Italian immigrant to England
- **Jacques Cartier (1534–1542)** — French explorer; made three voyages; the word **"kanata"** (meaning "village") became **"Canada"**

---

## New France (1600s–1700s)

- **Samuel de Champlain** — Founded Quebec City in **1608**; helped establish the first permanent French settlement; known as "Father of New France"
- **The fur trade** — Major economic driver; beaver pelts were highly valuable
- **Hudson's Bay Company** — Established **1670**; oldest commercial corporation in North America
- **Filles du roi** — Young women sent from France to help populate New France
- **Jean Talon** — First intendant of New France; promoted settlement
- **Count Frontenac** — Governor who fought to protect Quebec from English attacks
- **Coureurs des bois and voyageurs** — French fur traders and canoe travelers

---

## The Struggle for the Continent

- **Battle of the Plains of Abraham (1759)** — British General **Wolfe** defeated French General **Montcalm** near Quebec City; turning point in Canadian history
- **Treaty of Paris (1763)** — France ceded New France to Britain
- **Quebec Act (1774)** — Allowed French Canadians to keep their language, religion (Catholic), and civil law

---

## The Loyalists and Early Canada

- **United Empire Loyalists** — ~40,000+ people loyal to the British Crown fled the American Revolution to Canada
- Many Black Loyalists also settled in Nova Scotia; some later moved to Sierra Leone
- **Constitutional Act (1791)** — Split Quebec into **Upper Canada** (English, Ontario) and **Lower Canada** (French, Quebec)

---

## Key Milestones

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1793** | Upper Canada became **first province in the British Empire to move toward abolishing slavery** |
| **1807** | British Parliament abolished the Atlantic slave trade |
| **1833** | Slavery abolished throughout the British Empire |

---

## War of 1812

- Fought between Canada/Britain and the United States (1812–1815)
- **Key figures:**
  - **Sir Isaac Brock** — British military leader; died at the Battle of Queenston Heights
  - **Tecumseh** — Shawnee war chief; allied with the British
  - **Laura Secord** — Warned the British of an American attack
  - **Charles de Salaberry** — French-Canadian officer; led the Voltigeurs to victory at Châteauguay
- The war cemented **Canadian identity** and loyalty to the Crown

---

## Rebellions and Responsible Government

- **Rebellions of 1837–38** — Uprisings in Upper and Lower Canada against undemocratic governance
- **Lord Durham's Report (1839)** — Recommended responsible government and merging the Canadas
- **Sir Louis-Hippolyte La Fontaine** — Champion of democracy; helped achieve **responsible government (1847–49)**; first leader of a responsible government in the Canadas

---

## Confederation (1867)

- **July 1, 1867** — Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, and New Brunswick joined to form the **Dominion of Canada**
- **Sir John A. Macdonald** — **First Prime Minister** of Canada
- **Sir George-Étienne Cartier** — Key Father of Confederation from Quebec
- The new country had a **federal system** dividing powers between federal and provincial governments

## Expansion and the Railway

- **Louis Riel** — Métis leader; led resistance in Manitoba (1869–70) and Saskatchewan (1885); controversial figure — seen as both a hero and a rebel
- **Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR)** — Completed **1885**; linked Canada from coast to coast; symbolized **national unity**
- Thousands of Chinese laborers helped build the railway

---

## Sir Wilfrid Laurier

- **First French-Canadian Prime Minister** (1896–1911)
- Known for encouraging immigration to settle the West
- Famous for saying he wanted Canada to become a great nation

---

## World War I (1914–1918)

- Over **600,000 Canadians served**; approximately **60,000 killed**
- **Battle of Vimy Ridge (April 9, 1917)** — All four Canadian divisions attacked together for the first time; major victory; considered a defining moment of Canadian nationhood
- **April 9 = Vimy Day** (national holiday)
- Canada entered the war as part of the British Empire

---

## Women's Suffrage

- **Manitoba** was the first province to grant women the right to vote (**1916**)
- **Federal voting rights** for women came in **1918**
- **Agnes Macphail** — First woman elected to Parliament (1921)

---

## World War II (1939–1945)

- More than **1 million Canadians served**; approximately **44,000 killed**
- **D-Day: June 6, 1944** — Canadians stormed **Juno Beach** in Normandy, France
- Canada helped **liberate the Netherlands** from Nazi occupation
- **Battle of the Atlantic** — Longest continuous battle; Royal Canadian Navy and Air Force played key roles
- **Japanese-Canadian internment** — Japanese Canadians relocated from the Pacific coast; **government apologized in 1988**
- After WWII, Canada emerged as a **middle power** with growing international influence
